# Client Enquiry Email Templates
## The 3 Emails Around Every New Enquiry
### For Pet Photographers — by Ina J Photography

These 3 emails cover the complete enquiry journey:
1. First reply (the one that sets the tone)
2. The guide send (delivering your lead magnet)
3. The gentle follow-up (for when it goes quiet)

Every template below is a **prompt** — paste it into Claude with `/my-voice` active and it will write the email in your voice, personalised to the client.

Or use them as starting points and edit directly.

---

**Before you use these:**
Make sure your CLAUDE.md is up to date. Claude will read it to personalise every email with your name, location, offer details, and brand voice.

## EMAIL 1: First Reply to a New Enquiry

### When to send: Within a few hours of receiving the enquiry.

### The prompt:

```
You're writing the first reply email for [your name], a pet photographer based in [your town].

Read my CLAUDE.md — my voice, my offer, my ideal client, my process.

New enquiry details:
- Client name: [name]
- Their dog: [name and breed if mentioned]
- What they said: [paste or summarise their enquiry]
- Any specific dates or details they mentioned: [if any]

Write my first reply.

This email should:
- Open by acknowledging something specific they said (their dog's name, what they're after, why they reached out)
- Feel warm and like a real person wrote it, not a template
- Briefly give them a sense of what a session with me is like (1 to 2 sentences, not a full pitch)
- Tell them the clear next step (how to book a call, how to check availability, what to do now)
- Be short — 3 paragraphs maximum

Rules:
- My voice from CLAUDE.md — not corporate, not over-enthusiastic
- Reference their dog by name if they mentioned it
- No "I'd love to work with you" or "I'd be honoured" (everyone says this)
- Sign off naturally in my voice
- Australian English if I'm Australian

One version. Make it feel real.
```

### What good looks like:

The email should feel like a text from a photographer who's genuinely excited about their dog — not a form letter. If it could have come from any photographer, rewrite it.

## EMAIL 2: Sending Your "What to Expect" Guide

### When to send: Either in the first reply, or as a follow-up once they've expressed interest.

### The prompt:

```
You're writing an email for [your name] that sends a client their "What to Expect from Your Pet Photography Session" guide.

Read my CLAUDE.md — my voice, my offer, my process.

Client details:
- Name: [name]
- Their dog: [name/breed if known]
- Where they're at: [just enquired / have had a chat / are thinking about booking]

Write the email that delivers the guide.

This email should:
- Feel like a helpful gift, not a marketing send
- Give them 1 to 2 lines on why I put this guide together (what it will help them with)
- Tell them what's in it without over-explaining
- Include a soft invitation to book or get in touch once they've read it
- End with a natural sign-off

The guide link or attachment will be added separately — just write [GUIDE LINK HERE] as a placeholder.

Rules:
- Short. 3 paragraphs maximum.
- My voice. Warm and practical.
- Not pushy. This email is helpful, not salesy.
- Reference their dog if I know it

One version.
```

### A note on delivering the guide:

Once you've built your "What to Expect" guide (lesson 4), you can:
- Copy the text into the email body
- Convert the HTML file to PDF and attach it
- Share it as a Google Doc link
- Host it as a page on your website

The email above works for any of these — just swap in the right link or delivery method.

## EMAIL 3: The Gentle Follow-Up

### When to send: 3 to 7 days after your first reply, if you haven't heard back.

### The prompt:

```
You're writing a gentle follow-up email for [your name].

Read my CLAUDE.md — my voice and offer.

Context:
- I emailed [client name] [timeframe] ago — first reply to their enquiry
- Their dog: [name/breed if known]
- What they enquired about: [brief note]
- They haven't replied

Write a short follow-up email that checks in without feeling like a sales chase.

This email should:
- Not open with "just following up" or "circling back"
- Feel casual and genuine, not automated
- Mention something specific about their dog or enquiry so it doesn't read as a mass send
- Leave the door open without pressure
- Be very short — 2 paragraphs maximum

Rules:
- My voice
- No urgency tactics, no scarcity ("I have one spot left")
- Warm. Assumes the best (they're busy, not uninterested)
- Easy for them to reply to — one clear, low-pressure sentence they can respond to

One version.
```

### When to send a second follow-up (and when to stop):

If they don't reply to the gentle follow-up either, one more touch is fine:
- A week or two later
- Even shorter — one sentence checking in, no agenda
- After that, let it go

Chasing further than that damages the relationship before it's started.

## BONUS: The "We're Fully Booked" Reply

### When to send: When you're at capacity and someone enquires.

### The prompt:

```
You're writing a reply for [your name] to someone who has just enquired but you're currently fully booked.

Read my CLAUDE.md — my voice and offer.

Enquiry details:
- Client name: [name]
- Their dog: [name/breed if known]
- Timeframe they mentioned: [if any]

Write a reply that:
- Thanks them genuinely (not in a template way)
- Tells them clearly you're fully booked for [timeframe]
- Invites them to join your waitlist or reach out again in [specific month]
- Leaves them feeling good about you, not brushed off

Rules:
- Short. 2 to 3 paragraphs.
- My voice
- Warm and specific, not generic
- Give them a concrete next step (what to do if they want to be notified, or when to check back)

One version.
```

## MAKING THESE EVEN BETTER

**Add your guide to every first reply.** Don't wait to send the guide — include it in email 1. Clients who read it before the booking call convert better.

**Personalise the opening line every time.** Claude will do this for you if you paste the enquiry details. A personalised first line is the difference between a reply that gets read and one that gets filed.

**Save the versions that work.** When Claude writes an email that converts particularly well — client books, thanks you for the warm reply — save that version. Note what made it land.

**Check the voice after every draft.** Before you send, re-read it out loud. If it sounds like a photographer wrote it, send it. If it sounds like a template, ask Claude: "What in this email sounds like AI? Rewrite those parts in my voice."
