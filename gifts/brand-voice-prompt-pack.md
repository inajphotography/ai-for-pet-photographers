## INSTAGRAM CAPTIONS

### Prompt 1: Session Story Caption

```
You're writing an Instagram caption for [their name], a pet photographer.

Read their brand voice profile from CLAUDE.md before writing a single word.

The session detail:
[Describe what happened — the dog's name, breed, a specific moment, the light, where you were, something the dog did that made you smile]

Write a caption that leads with that moment and ends with an invitation to enquire.

Rules:
- Match their sentence rhythm exactly
- Use their actual words and phrases, not generic pet photography language
- No exclamation marks unless they use them
- No "capturing memories", "furry family member", or "magical moments"
- Length: whatever feels right in their voice, not longer just to be longer
- No hashtags in the caption body
- One genuine call to action at the end, low pressure

Write one version. Make it real.
```

### Prompt 2: Emotional Moment Caption

```
You're writing an Instagram caption for [their name].

Read their CLAUDE.md brand voice profile first.

The moment:
[Describe an emotional or connecting moment — an old dog, a dog and their owner, a dog doing something that surprised you]

This caption should sit with the feeling for a moment before it moves anywhere.
Don't resolve it quickly. Let it breathe.

Rules:
- Their rhythm, their words
- Warm but not sentimental in a performative way
- No mortality language ("they won't be here forever", "cherish every moment")
- Lead with the dog or the moment, not with a question to the reader
- Length: short to medium
- No hashtags in the body

One version. Write it like you mean it.
```

### Prompt 3: Behind the Scenes Caption

```
You're writing an Instagram caption for [their name].

Read their CLAUDE.md brand voice first.

The BTS moment:
[What happened behind the scenes — prep, a funny moment, something the client said, what you were thinking, the chaos before the perfect shot]

This caption should feel like a window into how [their name] actually works.

- Their personality should come through, not just information
- Brief. This isn't an essay.
- End with something that invites them in, not a hard sell

One version.
```

## ENQUIRY AND CLIENT EMAILS

### Prompt 5: First Reply to a New Enquiry

```
You're writing an email for [their name], a pet photographer.

Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their offer, their process.

A new enquiry has just come in. The client mentioned:
[What the client said — their dog's name, what they're looking for, any relevant details]

Write the first reply email.

This email should:
- Feel warm and personal, not templated
- Reference something specific they said (their dog's name, what they're after)
- Briefly acknowledge what a session with [their name] looks like
- Include the next step (how to book, what to do now)
- Be short. 3 to 4 paragraphs maximum.

Rules:
- Their voice. Not corporate. Not over-the-top enthusiastic.
- Australian English if they're Australian
- No "I'd love to work with you" (everyone says this)
- Sign off in their natural way

Write one version.
```

### Prompt 6: Re-engaging a Gone-Quiet Enquiry

```
You're writing an email for [their name].

Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice and offer details.

Someone enquired [timeframe] ago and hasn't replied since the first email.
Their dog was: [breed/name if known]
What they were interested in: [type of session or what they said]

Write a short follow-up email that:
- Doesn't feel like a sales chase
- Checks in genuinely
- Mentions one specific thing from their original enquiry if possible
- Leaves the door open without any pressure

Rules:
- Short. 2 paragraphs maximum.
- Their voice
- No "just following up" as an opener
- No urgency tactics or scarcity language

One version.
```

### Prompt 7: Session Confirmation and Reminder Email

```
You're writing an email for [their name].

Read their CLAUDE.md — their process, their voice, what to expect details.

A client has just confirmed their booking. Session details:
- Date: [date]
- Location: [location]
- Dog: [name and breed]

Write a session confirmation email that:
- Confirms the details clearly
- Tells them exactly what to bring and how to prepare (use their CLAUDE.md process details)
- Gets them excited about the session
- Reassures them if they're nervous (many first-timers are)

Rules:
- Warm and practical
- Their voice throughout
- Not too long — clients skim emails
- No jargon

One version.
```

### Prompt 9: Testimonial Request Email

```
You're writing an email for [their name].

Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice.

A client received their gallery [timeframe] ago and [their name] wants to ask for a review or testimonial.

Write the testimonial request email.

This email should:
- Not feel transactional or templated
- Reference their specific session in a real way
- Make it easy for them to know what to say (give them 2 to 3 prompts they could answer)
- Keep the ask gentle and low-pressure

Rules:
- Short. 3 paragraphs maximum.
- Their voice. Conversational.
- No "it would mean the world to me" or excessive gratitude
- Give them a direct link or clear instruction for where to leave the review

One version.
```

## REELS AND VIDEO CONTENT

### Prompt 10: Reel Hook + Script

```
You're writing a Reel script for [their name], a pet photographer.

Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their ideal client, their style.

Topic: [what the Reel is about — a tip, a myth, a behind-the-scenes, a transformation, a story]

Write:
1. A hook (first 3 seconds — 1 sentence, said out loud, that stops the scroll)
2. The body (5 to 7 short sentences that deliver the value or story)
3. A closing line (not a hard CTA — something that lands the point)

Rules:
- Hook must create immediate tension, curiosity, or recognition ("I know this feeling")
- Written to be spoken out loud — no complex sentences
- Their voice. If they're naturally funny, let it be funny. If they're warm and real, keep it that way.
- No "this is your sign", "you need to hear this", "POV: you..."
- No pointing to text on screen instructions — just the script
- Total length: 30 to 45 seconds when spoken at normal pace

One version. Script format.
```

### Prompt 11: Talking-to-Camera Script (Educational)

```
You're writing a short video script for [their name].

Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their ideal client, their offer.

Topic: [something their ideal client would genuinely want to know — a prep tip, a common mistake, what to expect, why dogs need a specific type of session, etc.]

Write a 60-second talking-to-camera script.

Structure:
1. Hook (one sentence, said out loud)
2. The thing (2 to 3 key points, spoken simply)
3. Close (one line that either connects back to their service or just lands the idea well)

Rules:
- Conversational. Simple words. No jargon.
- Their voice throughout
- Written to be spoken, not read
- Helpful first, promotional nowhere
- Literally 60 seconds when read at a normal pace

One version.
```

## BLOG AND WEB CONTENT

### Prompt 12: Blog Post Introduction

```
You're writing the opening paragraphs of a blog post for [their name].

Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their ideal client, their style.

Blog post topic: [topic]
Target reader: [who they're writing for — dog mums, first-time clients, people considering booking, etc.]

Write the opening 2 to 3 paragraphs only.

This opening should:
- Drop the reader into a real, specific moment or scenario they recognise
- Connect that moment to what the post will answer
- Sound like [their name] is talking to a friend, not writing for a content machine

Rules:
- Their voice
- No "In today's post, we'll explore..."
- No "Are you a dog mum who..." as the opener
- Specific over generic
- Maximum 150 words

One version.
```

### Prompt 13: Website Homepage Tagline

```
You're writing a homepage tagline for [their name].

Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their ideal client, their style, their location.

The tagline needs to:
- Say what they do and who they do it for in one line
- Feel like something [their name] would actually say, not a marketing headline
- Be specific to their location or their ideal client if possible

Give me 5 options. Different approaches: some direct, some emotional, some specific.

Rules:
- No more than 10 words each
- No: "capturing memories", "timeless portraits", "authentic moments"
- Their voice
- Options that they'd actually put on their website, not options that sound like placeholders

Five options.
```

### Prompt 14: Instagram Bio Rewrite

```
You're rewriting the Instagram bio for [their name].

Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their location, their ideal client, their style.

Current bio (if they have one):
[paste it or say "none yet"]

Write a new bio that:
- Immediately tells the right person they're in the right place
- Sounds like a real human wrote it
- Includes location (city)
- Has a clear CTA (link in bio, DM, book, enquire)
- Fits the 150 character limit

Give me 3 options with slightly different angles.

Rules:
- No emoji unless they use emoji in their voice
- No "capturing" anything
- Their voice, not generic pet photographer language
- Actually counts the characters — must fit 150

Three options.
```


## HOW TO MAKE THESE EVEN BETTER

**Stack them.** After the caption is drafted, say: "Now read the caption you just wrote and check it against my brand voice profile. What would you change?"

**Audit them.** Ask Claude: "What in this caption sounds like AI, not like me? Fix those parts."

**Save your wins.** When Claude writes something that really sounds like you, save the prompt + output together. That becomes your training data for the next version of your voice command.

**Update your voice file.** Every few months, paste new captions into `/my-voice` or update your CLAUDE.md brand voice section. Your voice evolves. Keep the file current.
