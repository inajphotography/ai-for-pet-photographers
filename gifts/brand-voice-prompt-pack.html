Category 01

Instagram Captions

Prompt 01

Session Story Caption

You're writing an Instagram caption for [their name], a pet photographer. Read their brand voice profile from CLAUDE.md before writing a single word. The session detail: [Describe what happened — the dog's name, breed, a specific moment, the light, where you were, something the dog did that made you smile] Structure the caption in 3 parts: 1. HOOK — first 1 to 2 lines. Must stop the scroll. A specific detail, a question, or an observation that makes someone pause. Never start with "I" or a generic statement. 2. VALUE / STORY — deliver the moment. Let the image and the story breathe. This is where the connection happens. 3. CTA — one line. Choose the right one for the goal: - Bookings: "Sessions are open — link in bio to enquire" - Engagement: "Comment SESSION and I'll send you the details" - Saves: "Save this if you're thinking about booking a session for [dog name]" - Shares: "Share this with someone whose dog deserves this" - Connection: "Tell me about your dog below" Rules: - Match their sentence rhythm exactly - Use their actual words and phrases, not generic pet photography language - No exclamation marks unless they use them - No "capturing memories", "furry family member", or "magical moments" - No hashtags in the caption body - The CTA must match the purpose — don't use a bookings CTA on an educational post Write one version. Make it real.
Prompt 02

Emotional Moment Caption

You're writing an Instagram caption for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md brand voice profile first. The moment: [Describe an emotional or connecting moment — an old dog, a dog and their owner, a dog doing something that surprised you] Structure the caption in 3 parts: 1. HOOK — open with the moment itself. A specific visual, a behaviour, something the dog did. Not a question, not "this is why I do what I do." Just the moment. 2. VALUE / STORY — sit with the feeling. Let it develop. Don't rush to the lesson or the point. 3. CTA — one gentle line. For this type of post, connection works better than bookings: - "Tell me about your dog below" - "Save this one" - "Tag someone who needs to see this" Rules: - Their rhythm, their words - Warm but not sentimental in a performative way - No mortality language ("they won't be here forever", "cherish every moment") - No hashtags in the body - The CTA should feel like a natural end to the story, not a gear-shift One version. Write it like you mean it.
Prompt 03

Behind the Scenes Caption

You're writing an Instagram caption for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md brand voice first. The BTS moment: [What happened behind the scenes — prep, a funny moment, something the client said, what you were thinking, the chaos before the perfect shot] Structure the caption in 3 parts: 1. HOOK — open with something specific that happened. The chaos, the funny line, the thing that didn't go to plan. Not "here's what goes on behind the scenes." 2. VALUE / STORY — let them into the moment. Make them feel like they're watching it happen. 3. CTA — for BTS posts, curiosity and engagement work best: - "Comment SESSION if you want to see what it's like to book with me" - "Save this if you've been on the fence about booking" - "Tell me your dog's chaos mode below" Rules: - Conversational. First person. Present tense if it works. - Their personality should come through, not just information - Brief. This isn't an essay. - CTA should match the energy of the post One version.
Prompt 04

Breed Feature Caption

You're writing an Instagram caption for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md first. Write a caption about photographing [specific breed or dog type]. Include one specific thing that makes photographing this breed interesting, challenging, or joyful — from [their name]'s actual experience. Structure the caption in 3 parts: 1. HOOK — open with the breed-specific truth. Something that only a photographer who has worked with this breed would know. Specific, not generic. 2. VALUE / STORY — the experience of photographing them. One real moment or observation. 3. CTA — choose based on goal: - Bookings: "Got a [breed]? Link in bio to enquire about a session" - Engagement: "Tell me about your [breed] below — I want to hear everything" - ManyChat: "Comment [BREED] and I'll send you what to expect when photographing them" Rules: - Specific, not generic - Their voice and rhythm - Don't write like a dog encyclopedia - Medium length One version.
Category 02

Enquiry & Client Emails

Prompt 05

First Reply to a New Enquiry

You're writing an email for [their name], a pet photographer. Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their offer, their process. A new enquiry has just come in. The client mentioned: [What the client said — their dog's name, what they're looking for, any relevant details] Write the first reply email. This email should: - Feel warm and personal, not templated - Reference something specific they said (their dog's name, what they're after) - Briefly give them a sense of what a session is like (1 to 2 sentences, not a full pitch) - Include the next step (how to book, what to do now) - Be short — 3 paragraphs maximum Rules: - Their voice from CLAUDE.md — not corporate, not over-enthusiastic - Reference their dog by name if they mentioned it - No "I'd love to work with you" or "I'd be honoured" (everyone says this) - Sign off naturally in their voice Write one version. Make it feel real.
What good looks like: The email should feel like a text from a photographer who's genuinely excited about the dog — not a form letter. If it could have come from any photographer, rewrite it.
Prompt 06

Re-engaging a Gone-Quiet Enquiry

You're writing an email for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice and offer details. Someone enquired [timeframe] ago and hasn't replied since the first email. Their dog was: [breed/name if known] What they were interested in: [type of session or what they said] Write a short follow-up email that: - Doesn't feel like a sales chase - Checks in genuinely - Mentions one specific thing from their original enquiry if possible - Leaves the door open without any pressure Rules: - Short. 2 paragraphs maximum. - Their voice - No "just following up" as an opener - No urgency tactics or scarcity language - Warm. Assumes they're busy, not uninterested. - Confident — not apologetic for following up One version.
Prompt 07

Session Confirmation Email

You're writing an email for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md — their process, their voice, their session details. A client has just confirmed their booking. Session details: - Date: [date] - Location: [location] - Dog: [name and breed] Write a session confirmation email that: - Confirms the details clearly - Tells them what to bring and how to prepare (use their CLAUDE.md process details) - Gets them genuinely excited about the session - Reassures them if they're nervous Rules: - Warm and practical - Their voice throughout - Not too long — clients skim emails - No jargon One version.
Prompt 08

Gallery Is Ready Email

You're writing an email for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md — their delivery process, their voice. A client's gallery is ready. Details: - Client name: [name] - Their dog: [name and breed] - Session type: [outdoor/studio/lifestyle] - One thing that made this session special: [specific detail] Write the "your gallery is ready" email. This email should: - Feel like a mini reveal, not just a notification - Reference something specific from their session - Tell them clearly how to access their gallery and what to do next - If they do IPS, mention next steps for artwork Rules: - Short and warm - Their voice - One genuine moment of enthusiasm — not fake enthusiasm - Clear next step One version.
Prompt 09

Testimonial Request Email

You're writing an email for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice. A client received their gallery [timeframe] ago and [their name] wants to ask for a review or testimonial. Write the testimonial request email. This email should: - Not feel transactional or templated - Reference their specific session in a real way - Make it easy for them to know what to say (give them 2 to 3 prompts they could answer) - Keep the ask gentle and low-pressure Rules: - Short. 3 paragraphs maximum. - Their voice. Conversational. - No "it would mean the world to me" or excessive gratitude - Give them a direct link or clear instruction for where to leave the review One version.
Category 03

Reels & Video Content

Prompt 10

Reel Hook + Script

You're writing a Reel script for [their name], a pet photographer. Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their ideal client, their style. Topic: [what the Reel is about — a tip, a myth, a behind-the-scenes, a transformation, a story] Write: 1. A hook (first 3 seconds — 1 sentence, said out loud, that stops the scroll) 2. The body (5 to 7 short sentences that deliver the value or story) 3. A closing line (not a hard CTA — something that lands the point) Rules: - Hook must create immediate tension, curiosity, or recognition - Written to be spoken out loud — no complex sentences - Their voice. If they're naturally funny, let it be funny. If they're warm and real, keep it that way. - No "this is your sign", "you need to hear this", "POV: you..." - Total length: 30 to 45 seconds when spoken at normal pace One version. Script format.
Prompt 11

Talking-to-Camera Script (Educational)

You're writing a short video script for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their ideal client, their offer. Topic: [something their ideal client would genuinely want to know — a prep tip, a common mistake, what to expect, why dogs need a specific type of session, etc.] Write a 60-second talking-to-camera script. Structure: 1. Hook (one sentence, said out loud) 2. The thing (2 to 3 key points, spoken simply) 3. Close (one line that lands the idea or connects back to their service) Rules: - Conversational. Simple words. No jargon. - Their voice throughout - Written to be spoken, not read - Helpful first, promotional nowhere - Literally 60 seconds when read at a normal pace One version.
Category 04

Blog & Web Content

Prompt 12

Blog Post Introduction

You're writing the opening paragraphs of a blog post for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their ideal client, their style. Blog post topic: [topic] Target reader: [who they're writing for — dog owners, first-time clients, people considering booking, etc.] Write the opening 2 to 3 paragraphs only. This opening should: - Drop the reader into a real, specific moment or scenario they recognise - Connect that moment to what the post will answer - Sound like [their name] is talking to a friend, not writing for a content machine Rules: - Their voice - No "In today's post, we'll explore..." - No "Are you a dog owner who..." as the opener - Specific over generic - Maximum 150 words One version.
Prompt 13

Website Homepage Tagline

You're writing a homepage tagline for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their ideal client, their style, their location. The tagline needs to: - Say what they do and who they do it for in one line - Feel like something [their name] would actually say, not a marketing headline - Be specific to their location or their ideal client if possible Give me 5 options. Different approaches: some direct, some emotional, some specific. Rules: - No more than 10 words each - No: "capturing memories", "timeless portraits", "authentic moments" - Their voice - Options they'd actually put on their website, not placeholder copy Five options.
Prompt 14

Instagram Bio Rewrite

You're rewriting the Instagram bio for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their location, their ideal client, their style. Current bio (if they have one): [paste it or say "none yet"] Write a new bio that: - Immediately tells the right person they're in the right place - Sounds like a real human wrote it - Includes location (city) - Has a clear CTA (link in bio, DM, book, enquire) - Fits the 150 character limit Give me 3 options with slightly different angles. Rules: - No emoji unless they use emoji in their writing naturally - No "capturing" anything - Their voice, not generic pet photographer language - Must actually fit 150 characters Three options.
Prompt 15

Monthly Newsletter Opening

You're writing the opening of a monthly email newsletter for [their name]. Read their CLAUDE.md — their voice, their ideal client. Month / season: [current month or season] One real thing that happened this month in their work: [a session, a funny moment, something they noticed, something that moved them] Write the newsletter opening: 2 to 3 short paragraphs that: - Feel like catching up with a friend, not a business broadcast - Reference something real and specific from the month - Leave the reader wanting to keep reading Rules: - Their voice throughout - No "Happy [Month]!" openers - Short paragraphs - Warm and personal, not promotional - End the opening with a natural transition into whatever the newsletter is about One version.

Make these even better

Stack them.

After the caption is drafted, say: "Now read the caption you just wrote and check it against my brand voice profile. What would you change?"

Audit them.

Ask Claude: "What in this caption sounds like AI, not like me? Fix those parts."

Save your wins.

When Claude writes something that really sounds like you, save the prompt and output together. That becomes your training data for the next version of your voice command.

Update your voice file.

Every few months, paste new captions into /my-voice or update the brand voice section in your CLAUDE.md. Your voice evolves. Keep the file current.
