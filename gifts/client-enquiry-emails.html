Client Enquiry Email Templates

The 3 emails around every new enquiry — first reply, guide send, and gentle follow-up. Written in your voice, personalised to the client.

by Ina J Photography

Before you use these

Make sure your CLAUDE.md is up to date. Claude reads it to personalise every email with your name, location, offer, and brand voice.

How to use these prompts:

  1. Type /my-voice to load your brand voice command (from Lesson 2)
  2. Paste the prompt below with the client details filled in
  3. Read the output out loud before sending — if it sounds like a template, ask Claude to rewrite the parts that don't sound like you
Email 01

First Reply to a New Enquiry

When to send: Within a few hours of receiving the enquiry.

You're writing the first reply email for [your name], a pet photographer based in [your town]. Read my CLAUDE.md — my voice, my offer, my ideal client, my process. New enquiry details: - Client name: [name] - Their dog: [name and breed if mentioned] - What they said: [paste or summarise their enquiry] - Any specific dates or details they mentioned: [if any] Write my first reply. This email should: - Open by acknowledging something specific they said (their dog's name, what they're after, why they reached out) - Feel warm and like a real person wrote it, not a template - Briefly give them a sense of what a session with me is like (1 to 2 sentences, not a full pitch) - Tell them the clear next step (how to book, how to check availability, what to do now) - Be short — 3 paragraphs maximum Rules: - My voice from CLAUDE.md — not corporate, not over-enthusiastic - Reference their dog by name if they mentioned it - No "I'd love to work with you" or "I'd be honoured" (everyone says this) - Sign off naturally in my voice - Use my country's spelling One version. Make it feel real.
What good looks like: The email should feel like a message from a photographer who's genuinely interested in their dog — not a form letter. If it could have come from any photographer, ask Claude to rewrite it.
Email 02

Sending Your "What to Expect" Guide

When to send: Either in the first reply, or as a follow-up once they've expressed interest.

You're writing an email for [your name] that sends a client their "What to Expect from Your Pet Photography Session" guide. Read my CLAUDE.md — my voice, my offer, my process. Client details: - Name: [name] - Their dog: [name/breed if known] - Where they're at: [just enquired / have had a chat / are thinking about booking] Write the email that delivers the guide. This email should: - Feel like a helpful gift, not a marketing send - Give them 1 to 2 lines on why I put this guide together (what it will help them with) - Tell them what's in it without over-explaining - Include a soft invitation to book or get in touch once they've read it - End with a natural sign-off Write [GUIDE LINK HERE] as a placeholder for the guide link or attachment. Rules: - Short. 3 paragraphs maximum. - My voice. Warm and practical. - Not pushy. This email is helpful, not salesy. - Reference their dog if I know it One version.
How to deliver the guide Once you've built your "What to Expect" guide in Lesson 4, you can:
  • Copy the text into the email body
  • Convert the HTML file to PDF and attach it
  • Share it as a Google Doc link
  • Host it as a page on your website
The email above works for any of these — swap in the right link or delivery method.
Email 03

The Gentle Follow-Up

When to send: 3 to 7 days after your first reply, if you haven't heard back.

You're writing a follow-up email for [your name]. Read my CLAUDE.md — my voice and offer. Context: - I emailed [client name] [timeframe] ago — first reply to their enquiry - Their dog: [name/breed if known] - What they enquired about: [brief note] - They haven't replied Write a short follow-up email that checks in without feeling like a sales chase. This email should: - Not open with "just following up" or "circling back" - Feel casual and genuine, not automated - Mention something specific about their dog or enquiry so it doesn't read as a mass send - Leave the door open — one clear, low-pressure sentence they can respond to - Be very short — 2 paragraphs maximum Rules: - My voice - Confident, not apologetic. I'm not sorry for following up. - Assumes they're busy, not uninterested - No urgency tactics, no scarcity - No "totally fine if you're not ready" — warm but not a doormat One version.
When to send a second follow-up: If they don't reply, one more touch is fine — a week or two later, even shorter, one sentence with no agenda. After that, let it go. Chasing further than that damages the relationship before it's started.
Bonus Email

The "We're Fully Booked" Reply

When to send: When you're at capacity and someone enquires.

You're writing a reply for [your name] to someone who has just enquired but you're currently fully booked. Read my CLAUDE.md — my voice and offer. Enquiry details: - Client name: [name] - Their dog: [name/breed if known] - Timeframe they mentioned: [if any] Write a reply that: - Thanks them genuinely (not in a template way) - Tells them clearly you're fully booked for [timeframe] - Invites them to join your waitlist or reach out again in [specific month] - Leaves them feeling good about you, not brushed off Rules: - Short. 2 to 3 paragraphs. - My voice - Warm and specific, not generic - Give them a concrete next step (what to do if they want to be notified, or when to check back) One version.

Making these even better

Include your guide in every first reply.

Don't wait to send the guide — include it in Email 1. Clients who read it before the booking call convert better because they already feel like they know what to expect.

Personalise the opening line every time.

Claude will do this for you if you paste the enquiry details. A personalised first line is the difference between a reply that gets read and one that gets filed.

Save the versions that work.

When Claude writes an email that converts well — client books, thanks you for the warm reply — save that version. Note what made it land. That becomes your benchmark.

Check the voice after every draft.

Before you send, read it out loud. If it sounds like a photographer wrote it, send it. If it sounds like a template, ask Claude: "What in this email sounds like AI? Rewrite those parts in my voice."
