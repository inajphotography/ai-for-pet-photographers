# 30 Power Prompts for Pet Photographers

A gift from Ina Jalil — Pet Photographers Collective

These aren't generic "write me a caption" prompts. Each one is engineered with role priming, context, constraints, and a quality bar so Claude gives you output that actually sounds like you, speaks to your ideal client, and is ready to use with minimal editing.

---

## How to Use Them

1. **Open Claude Code in the same folder as your CLAUDE.md.** That's the file you built in Lesson 1. Claude reads it automatically every time, which is why these prompts work better than anything you've copy-pasted into ChatGPT before.

2. **Copy the whole prompt** (not just the first line). The role and constraints are doing the work.

3. **Fill in the [brackets]** with your real details. The more specific you are, the better the output.

4. **Read the result, then refine.** If it doesn't sound like you, tell Claude:
> "Less polished, more conversational. Match my voice from CLAUDE.md. Cut anything that sounds like a stock photographer caption."

Claude gets closer every time you correct it.

## CONTENT IDEAS (Captions, Reels, Carousels)

### 1. Five Instagram captions for the week

Act as a senior social media strategist who specialises in creatives, with a background in consumer psychology and neuroscience. Using my brand voice and ideal client from my CLAUDE.md, give me 5 Instagram caption ideas for this week:

- 2 educational (teach my ideal client one thing they don't know about pet photography or printed artwork)
- 2 storytelling (a real session moment, a dog parent reaction, a behind-the-scenes feeling)
- 1 soft sell for my next available session

For each one, give me:
- A scroll-stopping hook (one line, designed to make a thumb stop)
- The body (40 to 100 words, in my voice)
- A CTA that earns a save, share, or comment (not a generic "DM me")
- 5 to 8 hashtags suited to my city and niche

**Quality bar:** A stranger should be able to tell my caption apart from a generic pet photographer's because of the specificity in voice, location, and the dog parent I'm speaking to.

### 2. Storytelling caption from a real session

Act as a pet photography copywriter who writes story-driven captions for high-end pet photographers. I just photographed [client first name]'s [dog name, breed, age, anything notable].

The session moment that stood out was: [describe one specific moment in 1 to 2 sentences]

Write me a caption that:
- Opens with a hook tied to the moment, not a generic line
- Tells the micro-story in 80 to 130 words, using my voice from CLAUDE.md
- Lands on why this matters for the dog parent, not me
- Ends with a soft, specific CTA (e.g. "tag a dog parent who'd want this for their dog before [season]")
- Includes 5 to 8 hashtags relevant to my location and ideal client

**Avoid:** "beautiful memories you'll treasure forever", "unleash", "unlock", "this is your sign", or any line that could appear in a stock pet photographer's caption.

### 3. 8-slide carousel: Why printed pet photography matters

Act as a content strategist who turns emotion-driven topics into shareable Instagram carousels for service businesses. Build me an 8-slide carousel on "Why printed pet photography matters" for my dog parent audience.

Use my brand voice from CLAUDE.md and the psychology of my ideal client (they treat their dog like family, they know dogs don't live as long as us, they value memory-keeping over things).

For each slide, give me:
- Slide number
- The headline (max 8 words, designed to be readable on a phone screen at a glance)
- The body copy (1 to 3 short lines max, conversational, no fluff)
- A direction for the visual (what kind of photo from my portfolio works best for this slide)

Slide 1 must be a scroll-stopper. Slide 8 must be a soft CTA, not a hard sell.

**Quality bar:** A dog parent should feel something before they think about booking.

## CONSULTATION + DISCOVERY

### 11. Pre-consultation prep

Act as a sales coach who specialises in service-based, relationship-driven offers. I have a consultation tomorrow with [first name]. They mentioned in their enquiry: [paste exactly what they said].

Give me:
- 5 questions I should ask in the first 10 minutes to understand what they actually want (not just what they put on the form)
- For each question, what the answer tells me about which experience to recommend
- 2 emotional cues to listen for that signal a high-fit client
- 1 question I should NOT ask (because it'll make them feel interviewed)

**Frame:** they're a dog parent with a dog they treat like family. They're considering investing real money in pet photography. They want to feel safe, understood, and excited. Not pitched.

### 12. Opening 5 minutes of a consultation

### 13. Objection handling: "I just want the digitals"

Act as a sales coach who specialises in helping service providers reframe objections without being pushy. A consultation client just said: "I really just want the digitals."

## PRICING CONVERSATIONS

### 15. Cold pricing question response

Act as a service business expert who specialises in moving pricing-curious leads into a real conversation. A new lead just asked: "How much do you charge?" before booking a consultation.

Write me a 2 to 4 line reply that:
- Acknowledges the question warmly
- Names a starting investment range (I'll fill in: my session fee starts at $[X], with most clients investing between $[Y] and $[Z] including artwork)
- Redirects to a quick consult so they can find the right fit
- Doesn't apologise, doesn't over-explain, doesn't sound corporate
- Ends with one specific next step (a calendar link, a question)

**Voice:** my CLAUDE.md. Should feel like a confident text from a friend who runs a real business.

Act as an email copywriter for premium service brands. Write the first email a new enquiry gets.

Goal: introduce my pricing range and the experience without scaring them off, so they show up to a consultation already half-sold.

**Voice from CLAUDE.md. Length:** 200 to 300 words. Easy to read on a phone.

## How to Make These Even Better

These prompts are already engineered. But you can compound them.

**Stack prompts.** After running prompt #1 (5 captions), say:
> "Now take caption #3 and turn it into a reel script using prompt #5's structure."

**Audit your output.** After Claude writes anything, ask:
> "Audit this against my CLAUDE.md voice. Where does it sound generic or like a stock photographer? Rewrite those lines."

**Train Claude on your real voice.** Paste 3 of your actual best-performing captions and ask:
> "Analyse the patterns in how I write — sentence length, opener style, emotional anchors, CTA style. Use that pattern when you write for me from now on."

**Save what works.** When Claude nails a piece, copy it into a `voice-examples.md` file in the same folder. Tell Claude to reference it. Your AI assistant gets sharper every time you do.
